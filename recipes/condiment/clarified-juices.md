# Clarified Juices

> [!NOTE] Origin
> Notebook page 3 clarified orange juice note. Two methods: acidic agar clarification and freeze-thaw clarification — both produce crystal-clear juice with concentrated flavor.

---

## Ingredients

*Base servings: 1 liter*

### Base Juice *(for both methods)*

- [ ] `1 liter (1000ml)` — fresh fruit or vegetable juice (e.g., orange, grapefruit, tomato, celery), strained to remove pulp
- [ ] *Optional: sweetener or acid to taste (adjust after clarification)*

### Method 1: Acidic Agar Clarification

- [ ] `2 grams` — agar-agar powder
- [ ] `20ml` — lemon juice or citric acid solution (to acidify to pH ~4.0)
- [ ] `2 tablespoons` — cold water (for blooming agar)

### Method 2: Freeze-Thaw Clarification

- [ ] *No additional ingredients; relies on freezing and thawing*

---

## Instructions

1. **Prepare the Base Juice.** Extract juice from fresh fruit or vegetables using a juicer or blender and strain through a fine-mesh sieve to remove pulp. Measure 1 liter of clear juice. Taste and adjust sweetness or acidity if desired — note that adjustments may affect clarification.

2. **Method 1: Bloom and Activate Agar.** In a small saucepan, sprinkle 2 grams agar-agar over 2 tablespoons of cold water and let bloom (~5 min). Add 20ml lemon juice (or citric acid solution) to the agar mixture. Heat over medium, stirring constantly, until the agar dissolves completely and the mixture reaches a boil (~1–2 min). Boil for 30 seconds to fully activate the agar.

3. **Method 1: Combine and Set.** Pour the hot agar mixture into the 1 liter of juice while whisking vigorously to ensure even distribution. The mixture should feel slightly thickened. Immediately pour into a shallow container and refrigerate for at least 2 hours, or until firmly set into a gel.

4. **Method 1: Break and Strain.** Once set, break the gel into coarse chunks using a fork or whisk. Place a cheesecloth-lined strainer over a bowl and pour the broken gel into the cloth. Let drain slowly under gravity for 1–2 hours — do not squeeze, as this can cloud the liquid. The liquid that drips through is the clarified juice, crystal clear and intensely flavored.

5. **Method 2: Freeze.** Pour the 1 liter of base juice into a freezer-safe container, leaving some headspace for expansion. Freeze solid, preferably overnight or at least 6 hours.

6. **Method 2: Thaw and Drain.** Remove the frozen juice block from the freezer and place it in a cheesecloth-lined strainer set over a bowl. Allow to thaw slowly in the refrigerator (or at room temperature if monitored) for 4–6 hours. As the ice melts, the liquid will drip through the cloth, leaving behind trapped solids and cloudiness. Collect the clarified liquid — it should be brilliantly clear.

7. **Final Storage.** For both methods, transfer the clarified juice to a clean, sealed bottle. Refrigerate and use within 3–5 days. The juice should be crystal clear with a concentrated, pure flavor.

---

## Notes & Variations

### Cook's Notes
- Agar clarification works best with juices that are naturally acidic or have been acidified; low-acid juices may require more lemon juice to achieve proper gelation.
- Freeze-thaw clarification is gentler and preserves more delicate aromatics, but requires freezer space and time; it may yield slightly less volume due to ice retention.

### Variations
- **Herb-Infused Clarified Juice:** Add a handful of fresh herbs (e.g., basil, mint) to the base juice before clarification for method 1; remove herbs before the agar step. For method 2, infuse after clarification.

### Make-Ahead
- Clarified juice keeps refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 5 days.
- For longer storage, freeze the clarified juice (not the unclarified) for up to 2 months; thaw in the refrigerator.

### Storage
- Store clarified juice in a clean, sealed bottle in the refrigerator for up to 5 days.
- Freeze for up to 2 months; thaw in the refrigerator before use.

### Scaling
- Both methods scale linearly; maintain the ratio of 2g agar per liter of juice for method 1.
- For method 1, ensure the agar is fully dissolved and boiled; for method 2, freezing time depends on container depth and freezer temperature.
