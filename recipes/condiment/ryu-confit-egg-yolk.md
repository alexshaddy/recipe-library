# Ryu Confit Egg Yolk

> [!NOTE] Origin
> Notebook note from the 5.13 Kat dinner page. Confit egg yolks gently cooked in oil at low temperature until custard-like, then finished with sesame and chili.

---

## Ingredients

*Base servings: 4 yolks*

- [ ] `4` — large egg yolks, separated and kept whole
- [ ] `1 cup` — neutral oil (such as grapeseed or canola), enough to fully submerge yolks
- [ ] `1 tablespoon` — toasted sesame oil
- [ ] `1/2 teaspoon` — gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)
- [ ] `1/4 teaspoon` — chili flakes (such as aleppo or crushed red pepper)
- [ ] `to taste` — flaky sea salt (for finishing)

---

## Instructions

1. **Separate the Yolks.** Carefully separate 4 egg yolks from the whites, keeping each yolk fully intact. Reserve whites for another use. Gently slide each yolk into a small bowl — handle with care; the membrane is delicate.

2. **Prepare the Oil.** In a small saucepan, combine neutral oil and sesame oil. The oil should be deep enough to fully submerge the yolks — about 1 inch. Insert a thermometer and warm over the lowest possible heat until the oil reaches 160°F/71°C. This takes patience; rushing will overshoot the temperature.

3. **Add the Yolks.** Once the oil is steady at 160°F/71°C, gently slide the yolks into the oil one at a time using a spoon. They should sink and sit on the bottom without touching each other. The oil will barely bubble — a sign the temperature is correct.

4. **Confit Slowly.** Maintain the oil at 160°F/71°C, adjusting heat as needed. Cook the yolks undisturbed for about 55 minutes. They are done when the surface turns opaque and firm to the touch but the center remains soft and custard-like — a gentle press should feel like a ripe peach. The yolks will take on a warm golden-orange hue.

5. **Remove and Drain.** Using a slotted spoon, carefully lift each yolk from the oil. Let excess oil drip off, then transfer to a paper towel-lined plate. The yolks will be fragile — handle them as you would a poached egg.

6. **Season and Serve.** Sprinkle the warm yolks with gochugaru, chili flakes, and a pinch of flaky sea salt. Serve immediately atop rice, ramen, grilled vegetables, or toast — the yolk should break open to reveal a silky, custard-like center.

## Notes & Variations

### Cook's Notes
- Temperature control is everything — if the oil exceeds 165°F/74°C, the yolks will firm up too quickly and lose their custard texture. Use a reliable thermometer and the lowest heat setting.
- The yolks can be confit directly in the serving oil; the sesame-chili infused oil left behind is excellent drizzled over rice or noodles.

### Variations
- **Soy-Cured Confit Yolk:** Before confit, cure the whole yolks in a mixture of 2 tablespoons soy sauce and 1 tablespoon mirin for 4 hours in the refrigerator. Pat dry, then confit as directed for a deeper umami finish.
- **Garlic-Infused Confit Yolk:** Add 2 smashed garlic cloves to the oil while heating; remove before adding the yolks for a subtle aromatic background.

### Make-Ahead
- Confit yolks can be made up to 2 days ahead. After cooking, transfer them to a small container and cover completely with fresh neutral oil. Refrigerate submerged.
- To serve, lift yolks from the oil and let them come to room temperature for 15 minutes, or warm gently in a 160°F/71°C water bath for 5 minutes.

### Storage
- Store confit yolks submerged in oil in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 2 days.
- Do not freeze — the texture will break down upon thawing.

### Scaling
- The recipe scales linearly; ensure yolks are fully submerged and not touching. Use a wider vessel for larger batches so the oil temperature remains even.
- For more than 6 yolks, increase the oil volume proportionally and monitor the temperature closely — more yolks will cool the oil slightly on addition.
